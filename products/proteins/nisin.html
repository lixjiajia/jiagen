Nisin

CAS No.: 1414-45-5  |  EINECS No.: 215-807-5

Synonyms: Lacticin 481, lantibiotic nisin, nisin A, nisin peptide, nisin Z

Product Summary

Nisin is a polycyclic antibacterial lantibiotic peptide derived from Lactococcus lactis. It functions by binding to lipid II and disrupting bacterial membranes, making it a powerful natural preservative in the food industry and a valuable tool in microbiological and pharmaceutical research. Nisin has a strong safety profile, broad activity against Gram-positive bacteria, and is increasingly applied in areas such as drug delivery, wound healing, and anti-biofilm strategies. It is a cornerstone in the study of antimicrobial peptides and biotechnology applications.

  • Formula: C143H230N42O37S7
  • Formula Molecular Weight: 3354.09

Function

Nisin inhibits growth of Gram-positive bacteria, particularly Listeria monocytogenes, Clostridium botulinum, and Bacillus spp. It is actively against a broad range of spoilage and pathogenic bacteria. Nisin is extensively used as food-grade biopreservative in meat, dairy, beverages, and canned foods. It is also used as an antimicrobial coating to prevents formation of bacterial biofilms in both clinical and food environments.

Mechanism of Action

  1. Pore Formation: Nisin binds to lipid II, a cell wall precursor, and inserts into bacterial membranes forming pores, causing ion leakage and cell death.
  2. Cell Wall Inhibition: Prevents synthesis of peptidoglycan by sequestering lipid II, thereby inhibiting bacterial growth.
  3. Synergistic Action: Often enhances effects of other preservatives or antibiotics.

Applications in Scientific Research

  • Food microbiology: Model compound for studying bacteriocin mechanisms and resistance.
  • Pharmaceutical research: Explored for topical and oral applications, especially for biofilm-associated infections.
  • Genetic engineering: The nisin-controlled expression (NICE) system is widely used for regulated protein expression in Lactococcus lactis.
  • Gut microbiome studies: Investigated for effects on intestinal flora and as an alternative to conventional antibiotics.
  • Emerging research frontiers:
    • Cancer therapy: Nisin’s mebrance-disrupting properties are explored for tumor-targeted drug delivery.
    • CRISPR-Cas integration: Nisin-induced promoters enable precise control of gene editing in synthetic biology circuits.

  • Store at 2 to 8°C.
