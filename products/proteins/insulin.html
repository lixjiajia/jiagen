Insulin (Porcine)

CAS No.: 12584-58-6  |  EINECS No.: 235-703-3

Synonyms: Insulin from porcine pancreas; insulin from hog pancreas; insulin (pork), insulin (swine)

Product Summary

Porcine insulin is the classic pancreas-derived peptide hormone used across metabolism research. It’s a 51-aa two-chain disulfide-bonded protein (C256H381N65O76S6; ~5.78 kDa) that differs from human insulin by a single B30 alanine. In cells and animals, it activates IR→PI3K/Akt signaling to drive GLUT4 translocation and anabolism, making it indispensable for 3T3-L1 adipogenesis, insulin tolerance testing, and canonical insulin-signaling experiments.

Biochemical Function

Porcine insulin functions exactly like human insulin, orchestrating nutrient uptake, glycogen and lipid storage, and protein synthesis, while actively suppressing glucose production, lipolysis, and proteolysis. It ensures that energy is conserved and stored after feeding, maintaining metabolic balance and growth.

Mechanism of Action

Insulin lowers blood glucose levels by binding to the insulin receptor (IR) on the surface of target cells, such as muscle, fat, and liver cells. This binding initiates a cascade of intracellular signaling events that lead to the translocation of glucose transporter 4 (GLUT4) vesicles to the cell membrane. The presence of GLUT4 on the membrane facilitates the uptake of glucose from the bloodstream into the cell, thereby reducing blood sugar levels.

Beyond glucose uptake, insulin also promotes the storage of energy by:

  • Stimulating the conversion of glucose into glycogen (glycogenesis) in the liver and muscle.
  • Enhancing the synthesis of fatty acids and triglycerides in adipose tissue.
  • Promoting protein synthesis and inhibiting their breakdown.

Applications in Scientific Research

  • Diabetes research: For many years, it was the primary treatment for diabetes, and its study helped establish the foundational understanding of insulin's role in the disease. Researchers still use it to investigate metabolic pathways and to serve as a reference standard for other insulin preparations.
  • Cell culture: It's often used as a supplement in cell culture media, particularly for cells that require high insulin levels for growth and differentiation. This includes adipocytes (fat cells) and muscle cells, as insulin promotes their growth and metabolic function.
  • Pharmacology and toxicology: Due to its similarity to human insulin, it's used in drug development and toxicological studies to evaluate the effects of new compounds on insulin sensitivity and glucose regulation in animal models.
  • Immunology: Its use in clinical practice highlighted the immune response to non-human insulin, leading to research into insulin antibodies and the development of recombinant human insulin to reduce immunogenicity.
